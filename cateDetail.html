Liangpi

Liangpi is a traditional snack commonly found in Qinghai and Northwest China. The production process is quite meticulous, involving first making dough from flour, then washing it repeatedly in water until the gluten and starch are separated. After the starch settles, it is steamed into liangpi, while the gluten can be used for cold dishes or stir-fries. Liangpi has a smooth, elastic texture and, when served with various condiments like garlic paste, chili oil, and sesame paste, it becomes even more delicious and diverse in flavor. Whether as a main dish or snack, liangpi will satisfy your taste buds. In Qinghai, people can often be seen holding bowls of liangpi, enjoying the tasty snack from Northwest China.

Historical Background and Cultural Significance

Liangpi's history dates back to ancient times, with folklore from the Shaanxi-Gansu region tracing it back to the Qin Shi Huang and Emperor Wu of Han periods. Liangpi is not only a delicacy but also a cultural heritage. In regions like Xinjiang, the production technique of liangpi was introduced by people from Shaanxi. This snack has become widely popular in Northwest China and is a favorite dish for cooling off during the hot summer days.

Taste and Eating Method

Liangpi has a delicate, smooth texture with a perfect balance of sourness and spiciness. To eat, cut the liangpi into thin strips, top it with steamed gluten slices, and drizzle with chili oil, vinegar, minced garlic, soy sauce, and mustard. The dish is visually appealing and fragrant.

Preparation Method

The preparation of liangpi involves several key steps:

1. Prepare and Rest the Dough: Mix high-gluten flour and salt, then gradually add water while stirring until a smooth dough forms. Cover with a damp cloth and let the dough rest for 30 minutes to 1 hour to allow the gluten to relax.

2. Wash the Gluten: Add water to the rested dough and knead it as if washing clothes, until the water turns cloudy and starch is washed out. Filter the starch water, repeating the process several times until the water is clear.

3. Settle the Starch: Let the collected starch water sit for 4-6 hours for the starch to naturally settle at the bottom. Avoid stirring or moving it during this time to ensure proper sedimentation.

4. Steam the Liangpi: Discard the top layer of clear water, leaving the starch paste at the bottom. Stir it well, then pour a suitable amount of starch paste into a greased flat dish. Steam it for 2-3 minutes until the liangpi puffs up, then remove it and cool it in cold water. Brush some oil on the surface to prevent sticking.

5. Slice and Add Seasoning: Cut the steamed liangpi into strips about 1 cm wide and place them in a bowl. Add your choice of toppings, such as cucumber strips and bean sprouts, then drizzle with chili oil, sesame paste, garlic paste, vinegar, soy sauce, and top with coriander to serve.

Qinghai

Qinghai, located in northwest China, boasts rich natural landscapes and profound cultural heritage, making it a charming and magical land. Here lies Qinghai Lake, the largest saltwater lake in China, with vast grasslands and majestic Qilian Mountains, forming an unparalleled natural landscape. Qinghai is not only one of the birthplaces of Tibetan culture, but also a paradise for nature enthusiasts and explorers.
