Qinghai

Qinghai Province (English name: Qinghai province), referred to as "Qing", is a provincial-level administrative region of the People's Republic of China. Located in the northeastern part of the Qinghai-Tibet Plateau in western China, it has jurisdiction over 2 prefecture-level cities and 6 autonomous prefectures. The total area is 722,300 square kilometers, with Xining as the provincial capital. As of the end of 2023, the permanent population of Qinghai is 5.94 million, with ethnic groups including Han, Tibetan, Hui, Mongol, and others. The commonly used languages are Chinese, Tibetan, Mongolian, and Salar.

Qinghai Province is located at the junction of major geological units in western China. The terrain is generally high in the west and low in the east, with a predominant landscape of plains and mountains. The climate is mainly plateau continental, with Qinghai Lake, one of the highest-altitude lakes in the world, located within the province. Thousands of years ago, ancient humans thrived in Qinghai. During the Shang and Zhou periods, Qinghai was referred to as the "Western Rong Qiang land". In the Han Dynasty, Emperor Wu established the counties of Linqiang and Poqiang in Qinghai. In the Tang Dynasty, Qinghai was governed by Tibet. Qinghai Province was officially established in January 1929.

Qinghai Province is one of the birthplaces of ancient Chinese civilization and an important source of Chinese culture. The Silk Road, Tangbo Ancient Road, and Tea Horse Road pass through Qinghai, forming a diverse culture centered around Kunlun culture, with contributions from various ethnic cultures. The province has 6 items listed on UNESCO's intangible cultural heritage list. Major tourist attractions include Qinghai Lake and Ta'er Monastery, both of which are rated 5A scenic spots in China.

Qinghai Province is an important ecological security barrier designated by the State Council and a key area for implementing the ecological protection and high-quality development strategy of the Yellow River Basin. It is an important dairy production region in western China and one of China's five major pastoral areas. Qinghai has been recognized by UNESCO as "one of the four major pollution-free ultra-clean zones in the world" and is known as the "Hometown of Chinese Tibetan Sheep" and the "Capital of World Yaks". In 2023, Qinghai Province's GDP reached 379.9 billion yuan, with a structure of 10.2% for primary industry, 42.4% for secondary industry, and 47.4% for tertiary industry.

Name Evolution

Qinghai Province is named after Qinghai Lake, the largest inland saltwater lake in China. In ancient times, Qinghai was known by several names, including Western Sea, Xianshuihai, and Beiheqianghai. The Tibetan name for Qinghai is Cuowenbo, and the Mongolian name is Kukunor, all of which refer to the blue-colored lake. During the Western Han period, Qinghai was referred to as Western Sea, derived from the name of the Qiang ethnic group living in the area. Over time, the name evolved, with various dynasties adapting it for use in their maps.

During the reign of Wang Mang, the name “Western Sea” began to be used more as a geographic reference. This shift continued through various periods of Chinese history, until Qinghai became its permanent name in the Northern Wei period.

Historical Evolution

Pre-Qin Period

As early as tens of thousands of years ago, ancient humans thrived in Qinghai. During the ancient times, the ancestors of the Qiang people migrated from the Jianghan region to Qinghai, living by the waters and practicing hunting and pastoralism. Before the Shang Dynasty, Qinghai was known as the "Three Dangerous Land". In the Xia Dynasty, the residents of the Hehuang region were called the Western Qiang, who were primarily engaged in animal husbandry, with the early stages of agriculture using oxen and horses. By the Western Zhou period, the local people had mastered the production of bronze weapons such as halberds and arrowheads, and made significant advances in agriculture and animal husbandry.

During the Warring States period, the influence of Central Plains culture prompted the Qiang people of the Hehuang region to shift from hunting and herding to agriculture. As agriculture developed, more tribes began to join this area, with the Qiang society implementing a hereditary leadership system.

Qin, Han to Northern and Southern Dynasties

During the Qin Dynasty, the Qinghai region experienced relative stability, providing favorable conditions for the growth of the Qiang population. Trade during this time was primarily based on bartering, with cattle playing an important role in trade. The introduction of copper smelting technology led to improvements in local production tools and daily items, promoting the development of handicrafts.

During the Western Han period, Qinghai was under the jurisdiction of Jincheng Commandery, with areas like Haowei, Poqiang, Anyi, and Linqiang counties, all within the Huangshui valley. Emperor Wu of Han expanded the Han Empire to the west, recovering the Hexi Corridor and restoring trade routes between Central and Western China. In 111 BC, General Li Xi and Langzhong Ling Xu Ziwei led forces into the Huangshui region and set up a military defense system.

In the year 61 BC, General Zhao Chongguo pacified the Qiang people’s border disputes and implemented the Tuntian system, establishing seven counties. As more Han Chinese people migrated to the area for farming, the mixing of Han and Qiang cultures further propelled the development of agriculture in the region. In the late Western Han period, Wang Mang set up the Western Sea Commandery in the present-day area of Sanjiao City, marking the formal integration of the eastern Qinghai region into the Chinese feudal administrative system.

After the Establishment of the People's Republic of China

On January 1, 1950, the Qinghai Provincial People's Government was formally established, with Xining as the capital. From 1953 to 1954, various Tibetan autonomous regions were formed, later renamed autonomous prefectures. From 1966 to 1976, during China's "Third Front" construction period, Qinghai became a key region for national development, significantly boosting urban growth.
